The SAIL Framework

Sawyer AI Leadership

SAIL: Not About Tools, About Judgment

S Social Intelligence

"How do we connect?"

Translate technical insights into compelling narratives for diverse audiences

A AI Literacy

"How does it work?"

Master AI tools with deep understanding of capabilities and limitations

I Innovation/Inquiry

"What's missing or wrong?"

Apply critical thinking beyond AI limitations using domain expertise

L Leadership

"Should we do this?"

Make principled decisions about AI implementation with ethical awareness

The Conductor Metaphor

Conductors don't play every instrument, but they know when to bring in the violins versus the brass section.

That's exactly what students learn with SAIL—determine which tool to use when, while directing the whole composition with human judgment.

Case Study: Prompt AIChemy

SAIL Framework in Action

What is Prompt AIChemy?

A competition where students become AI strategy consultants

Students craft AI prompts to extract strategic insights from real-world business datasets—demonstrating that effective AI use is about strategic orchestration, not technical coding.

Fall 2025: Boston Airbnb Analysis

  • ~4,500 real listings (September 2025 data)
  • Students hired as "consultants for Airbnb's Boston Market Development team"
  • Must balance competing stakeholder interests
  • 3 weeks, 3 strategic questions, endless insights

Design Principles

How We Built SAIL Into the Competition

Principle 1: Authentic Complexity

1 Real data, real stakeholders, real tensions

  • Real data: Boston Airbnb dataset (4,500 listings, current as of Sept 2025)
  • Real stakeholders: Hosts, guests, neighborhoods, city officials, Airbnb
  • Real ethical tensions: Housing affordability vs. economic opportunity
Why it matters: Students can't just optimize for one metric—they must navigate complexity and competing interests (just like real business)

Principle 2: Role-Based Framing

2 You're not "doing a project"—you're a consultant

"You are a data strategy consultant hired by Airbnb's Boston Market Development team. As Boston debates new short-term rental regulations, Airbnb needs strategic insights to guide their response—insights that balance business growth with community impact."
This shift in framing changes everything: Students think strategically about deliverables, audience, and business impact—not just "what the professor wants"

Principle 3: Structured Ambiguity

3 Clear requirements, multiple paths to excellence

What's Required:

  • Address all 3 questions
  • Document your prompts
  • Support claims with evidence
  • Consider stakeholder impacts

What's Open:

  • Which AI tools to use
  • Depth vs. breadth strategy
  • Analytical approach
  • Prompt engineering style
We grade the reasoning, not the "right answer"

Principle 4: SAIL Integration (Not Insertion)

4 SAIL emerges naturally from the design

Social Intelligence: Built in through multi-stakeholder analysis requirement

AI Literacy: Prompt documentation forces metacognition

Innovation/Inquiry: "Wait, that's weird" moments are explicitly valued in rubric

Leadership: Ethical considerations are central, not peripheral

"The ethical stuff wasn't a checkbox—it came naturally. When I discovered that rural sellers had longer delivery times, I immediately wondered about fairness in performance metrics and whether we were penalizing sellers for infrastructure issues beyond their control." — Spring 2025 Competition Winner

The Three Strategic Questions

Question 1: Host Success & Market Dynamics

"What factors distinguish successful hosts from struggling ones, and how can Airbnb help more hosts succeed?"

Question 2: Neighborhood Impact & Market Saturation

"Which Boston neighborhoods show healthy market conditions vs. potential over-saturation?"

Question 3: Guest Experience & Quality Assurance

"What drives exceptional guest experiences, and how can Airbnb identify quality issues early?"

What Makes These Questions Work?

  • Different analytical approaches: Q1 is segmentation, Q2 is geographic, Q3 is sentiment/quality
  • Different stakeholder perspectives: Hosts, neighborhoods, guests
  • Different ethical tensions: Individual success vs. community impact vs. platform reputation
  • Different communication needs: Technical analysis, strategic recommendations, ethical considerations
Together, they require ALL FOUR SAIL pillars—not as a checklist, but as a natural consequence of doing the work well

Evidence It Works: Student Outcomes

"I used to think AI either gave you the right answer or it didn't. But it's actually more like working with someone who needs really clear instructions and ethical guardrails. The better you are at asking questions and checking the results, the better it works." — Spring 2025 Participant
"My first prompts were terrible. I just started exploring and got better as I went. Don't wait for the perfect prompt—just start." — Spring 2025 Finalist

What We Observed (Spring 2025)

  • Students naturally raised ethical considerations without being prompted
  • Sophisticated prompt iteration emerged organically
  • Communication improved dramatically from first to final submission
  • Critical questioning of AI outputs became second nature
  • Students found patterns we (faculty) hadn't anticipated
When students naturally demonstrate all four SAIL pillars without being told to do so, that's not luck—that's brilliant learning design.

Domain Adaptations

How This Transfers Across Disciplines

The Template Pattern

[Domain] AIChemy

Dataset: Real, complex, current data
Role: Professional consultant framing
Question 1: Technical/analytical challenge
Question 2: Strategic/systemic analysis
Question 3: Ethical/stakeholder consideration

SAIL Integration: Emerges naturally from design
Assessment: Evaluate reasoning & judgment, not just answers

Finance Example: Portfolio AIChemy

Role: Consultant for university endowment

Dataset: 20 years of stock returns + economic indicators

Question 1: Risk-adjusted performance—What distinguishes resilient portfolios from fragile ones?

Question 2: Market regime detection—When do traditional correlations break down?

Question 3: Ethical investing—How to balance returns with ESG principles?

SAIL Hook: Students must present to both CFO (technical) and Board (strategic)

Marketing Example: Brand AIChemy

Role: Brand strategist for local Boston business

Dataset: Social media engagement + sales + demographic data

Question 1: Campaign effectiveness—What content drives conversion vs. awareness?

Question 2: Audience segmentation—Beyond demographics, what are behavioral tribes?

Question 3: Authentic engagement—How to use AI without losing human connection?

SAIL Hook: Must balance data-driven optimization with brand authenticity

Accounting Example: Audit AIChemy

Role: Audit analytics consultant

Dataset: Transaction records + vendor data + prior audit findings

Question 1: Anomaly detection—What patterns suggest fraud vs. errors?

Question 2: Risk assessment—Where should auditors focus limited time?

Question 3: Ethical automation—What should AI flag vs. require human judgment?

SAIL Hook: Must explain findings to both technical team and client executives

The Common Pattern

All three examples share:
  • Real data complexity
  • Multiple stakeholder perspectives
  • Ethical tensions built in
  • Communication challenge across audiences
  • No single "right answer"
The template works because it creates conditions where SAIL pillars emerge naturally

Key Takeaways

What We've Learned

  1. SAIL isn't just a framework we teach TO students—it's a design lens we use to CREATE learning experiences
  2. Authentic complexity drives authentic learning—real data, real tensions, real stakeholders
  3. Role-based framing transforms student mindset—from "what does the professor want?" to "what does the client need?"
  4. Assessment should evaluate judgment, not just technical execution
  5. The template is transferable—adapt it for your domain

The Meta-Lesson

You Don't Need to Be an AI Expert

You need to design experiences where students develop AI judgment through authentic practice.

That's something we, as faculty, already know how to do.
